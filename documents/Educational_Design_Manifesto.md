## The Educational Philosophy

### 1. What is exploration?

Exploration is the process of experimentation. It can be random, but it is not necessarily random. At some point, there are random choices made, but these are made cognitively, and informed. It is the choice of walk across the field or stay on the trail or turn around. There's a risk assessment - am I going to get stuck in the field, but at the same time that risk is mitigated by the potential rewards that one would find on that pathway. 

We explore for many reasons. We explore to be in the moment, to experience the thrill of discovery, but also to report back, authentically, and tell the story to others that we had that experience. Maybe we do not tell them directly that we explored code, per se, but we can show that we had this experience in the fluency that we approach a certain problem for code in the future.

We explore so that we may learn, too. We explore so that we can say we found the top of the mountain or the solution. Maybe it is to confirm that one way is possible. Maybe it is a well traveled peak that we explore so that we can say we did it too. Maybe we write the haiku so that we can explore the medium and be one of a long line of people who have written a hakiu. Maybe we explore a solution to MNIST, or the iris dataset not because it is a solved problem, but so that we can say that we too had that experience, and report to others and share that we are now part of that collective.

We explore for the stimulation and the novelty. To get lost in the woods and explore a trail you have taken 100 times before. We explore so that we can see there is a new tree. We explore so that we can learn the names of the planets, and the next time so that we can notice they have moved. We explore so that our understading of the world crystallizes, and that contributes to who we are.

### 2. Why is immediate feedback important?

Immediate feedback closes the loop. In systems engineering, there are open loop systems and closed loop. We need to feel the car move faster so that that we know the gas pedal works. We need to see the road so we do not count seconds and assume that we have reached the sop sign based on time. 

Every discrete moment is important if the loop is to be closed, and you know you are getting closer to the stop sign because you know the car is accelerating and you see the stop sign is approaching. The same can be said for the exploration of code - we know we are creating something beatuful and interesting and we can see the cause and effect because we see the changes we have made have hand an impact.

Humans are notoriously difficuly at separating causation. As a teacher, I see this daily. The students need to see the parts in isolation. Then they need to see mix of parts number 1, and mixture number 2. A studnet will see the mixture and the causailty if there is too much novelty at once.

Humans are barely able to handle linear causality, so when nonlinear or compound differential causality comes into play, there is little chance that the causailty can be determined in a single exposure. It must be iterative.

But to engage in the iteration requires willingness to explore. And willingness to explore requires the explorer to want to engage. If they simply go through the motions, there is no exploration, and as such there is no willingness to ponder and percieve the causality. 

### 3. Why are projects central?

A project is the coherent, cohesive package. When we go on a hike, the mission can be thought of as having a central "bucket" or buckets of goals; to see the view of the lake, to pass the lodge, etc. These goals come together in the form of the project and guide the exploration.

Without the framing of the project, you're all over the place. The project provides a framing so that your expectations are not to climb the glacier and meet the alligator in the forest on your trip to Mali. 

The project is a reality based framing of buckets that guides the exploration.

### 4. Why should code and artifacts live together?

Code should live alongside the artifacts to provide that feedback. We love to believe that people are better computer users than they are; they are not. Students tend not to understand file systems, locations and patterns the way we believe - and this is not their fault. Operating systems have worked to reduce the friction of computer use in an attempt to make computers accessible - but the artifact is that there is no longer need for 'computer use' classes. One can just pick up a computer and use it right away. But in this world, there is no sense of grounding - there are no folders, the terminal isnt' a different lens into the visual folders, and why would they need to be, since you can just search for everything? 

I'm a dinosaur, sure, but I value understading and my understanding is an artifact of my usage of the computer. In the same sense - I need the bridge between code and output to be as minimal as possible, so that with a solid foundation we can build actually complicated relationships.

### 5. Why should students retain work?

As an teacher, I fully understand that students will not retain lecture content. If I stand in front of a room and speak for 30 minutes, maybe bits and pieces will be retained by some individuals - but certainly not everyone will retain everything word for word, let alone understand on a single exposure.

Reflection is important feedback on the trail of discovery. Reflection is essential for discovery - but reflection can happen just in the course of active engagement. As you make notes in your brain of what you did to get there, with enough iteration you will learn. It doesn't necessarily require studying. But at a minimum, we need to see that trail of where we came through.

### 6. Why does authentic tooling matter?

If the point is to learn a concept, than an algebraic substitution is fine. But if the point is to do something more not really vocational, but useful and actually use the tool, you need to engage. At some point you need to do the thing. You need to take that risk. You need to see the beast in his own element, and not wonder what steps you were supposed to do to engage the beast, or get the beast to respond.

### 7. What is the difference between a toy and a creative instrument?

A toy is a fun approximation. A creative instrucment does not need too look fancy or be polished. MSPaint from windows 95 is not a toy; it can create pixel art that with transparency and build the sprites you need. 

A toy has a very narrowly defined approximatino of the tool. The toy is bounded. The capabilities of the toy can be explored, and it may provide some insight into how to use the tool. The tool will allow you to explore the tree of possibilities within the medium.
